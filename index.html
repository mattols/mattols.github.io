    About The Lab

    The Cryosphere Geospatial and Remote Sensing (CryoGeoSense) Lab at UVU studies how the frozen world of water is changing due to anthropogenic climate change and other human activities. Some questions we have include:

    • How is the Utah snowpack changing and what factors might accelarate changes we are seeing?
    • What is the distribution of ice left under heavy debris from the last glacial maximum in Utah?
    • What factors enhance or decrease the rate of glacial retreat in High Mountain Asia?
    • How can we better estimate and predict water resources using remote sensing, field observations, and numerical modeling?

    Dr. Olson mentors undergraduate students on projects that involve field, lab, and geospatial research. While some research focuses on remote sensing applications for other environmental or Earth systems research, most project revolve around hydrologic questions in snow- and ice-dominated watersheds in Utah and throughout the globe.

    Why care about alpine glaciers and snowpack?

    Glaciers and snow serve as essential water towers for downstream societal and ecological needs. As anthropogenic climate change alters the global energy balance we will continue to see rapid changes in terrestrial ice and water budgets throughout the globe.

    • 01 The cryosphere is a clear indicator of climate change
      The distribution of ice on planet Earth is linked to global climate. The global retreat of glaciers and shortening snow season indicate that changes are happening on our planet at a global scale.

    • 02 Sea-level and water resources

      Whether the concern is sea-level rise due to melting glaciers and ice sheets, or altering freshwater resources for downstream communities, the cryosphere is an important Earth system to study.

    • 03 Economy of snow and ice

      Utah's ski industry contributed $1.94 billion in nonresident visitor spending during the 2022-2023 ski season alone. Add the countless ski resort and mountain glacier attractions worldwide for a staggering global economy.

    • 04 We know more now than ever before

      Thanks to advances in satellite remote sensing, we have a clearer picture of our planet than ever before. We can observe the rapid retreat of glaciers and predict with numerical models how the cryosphere will change over the next century.

    • 05 Water is life

      Water and energy are the foundations of nearly all Earth's physical systems. Other water enthusiasts have said:

      "Thousands have lived without love, not one without water." — W. H. Auden.

      "We forget that the water cycle and life cycle are one." — Jacques Cousteau.

      "Water is the driving force of all nature." — Leonardo da Vinci.

    Projects

    Current Projects

    Dust on Snow

    Dust and other particulates on the snow surface enhance melt. How does dust deposition and how this affect the Utah snowpack? Collaboration with Sundance Ski Resort and the University of Utah.

    Utah Rock Glacier Mapping

    How much ice is left beneath thick debris cover throughout Utah and does it contribute to the water budget? Collaboration with the University of Utah and the Utah Geological Survey.

    Glacier Debris-cover Evolution

    As many glaciers in High Mountain Asia retreat, supraglacial debris cover expands, changing the rate at which glaciers melt.

    Wildfires and Snowpack

    Wildfires are becoming more frequent in Utah and the western US. How is the snow-water storage affected in post-fire landscapes?

    Isotopes and Flow

    Can stable water isotopes be used to improve water models? Collaboration with the US Geological Survey Water Science Center.

    Other Remote Sensing Work

    Various other student-led remote sensing research projects ranging from mineral detection with hyperspectral imagery to mapping agricultural changes in remote regions of Africa.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    About a Degree in Earth Science

    What can I do with a degree in Earth Science?

    Career opportunities in Earth science (geography, geology, and environmental science and management) are diverse and evolving. Graduates can pursue roles such as environmental consultant, hydrologist, remote sensing analyst, geological engineer, urban planner, climate analyst, air quality permit engineer, GIS specialist, and many more. These careers offer avenues for contributing to environmental sustainability, resource conservation, and understanding Earth's complex systems.

    What are essential skills that geoscience students should acquire during their undergraduate experience?

    Geoscience students should focus on developing a range of essential skills during their undergraduate studies. These include proficiency in data analysis and Geographic Information Systems (GIS), hands-on experience in fieldwork and laboratory techniques, and the ability to think critically and solve problems effectively. Strong communication and collaboration skills are crucial for presenting research findings and working in multidisciplinary teams. Additionally, technological literacy and a deep understanding of environmental awareness and ethics are essential for addressing contemporary geological and environmental challenges responsibly.

    What are essential skills for students pursuing a career in GIS and remote sensing?

    Students aspiring for careers in GIS and remote sensing should focus on developing essential skills such as technical proficiency in GIS software and remote sensing tools, spatial analysis techniques, and programming languages like Python and R. They should also prioritize gaining expertise in remote sensing interpretation, cartography, and data visualization, alongside database management skills. Critical thinking and problem-solving abilities are crucial for addressing complex spatial challenges across various sectors, and interdisciplinary knowledge in fields like geography and environmental science further enhances their versatility and applicability in diverse domains.

    How can an Earth Science student take full advantage of their undergraduate experience?

    To make the most of your undergraduate Earth Science program, diversify your coursework, engage in hands-on learning through fieldwork and research, and build strong relationships with professors and peers. Seek internships and experiences in relevant industries, develop technical skills in geospatial technologies and data analysis, and stay informed about the latest research and trends in the field. Consider contributing to research projects and exploring opportunities for graduate studies to further enhance your expertise and career prospects.

    How can students get involved in undergraduate research?

    Motivated students are encouraged to select a subject of personal interest for further exploration. Undergraduate research demands a significant investment of time and dedication, as students undertake independent inquiry under the supervision of a faculty mentor. It is advisable to establish connections with faculty members whose areas of expertise resonate with the student's academic pursuits and interests.

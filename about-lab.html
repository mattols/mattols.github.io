Research Approach

WILD Lab research integrates field, lab, and computing resources to address scientific questions about water, the cryosphere, and other land surface processes. This multifaceted approach includes data collection, computational analysis, and validation strategies to reveal more information about our changing environment. Read more below!

The WILD Lab

The "WILD" Lab stands for the Water, Ice, and Land Dynamics Lab. Our research focuses primarily on understanding water resources through a lens of snow and glacier hydrology and other landcover and ladscape changes through the use of field measurements, modeling, and remote sensing image analysis. We are concerned with how our planet is changing and focus research on better understanding how Earth systems are responding to climate and other anthropogenic disturbances.

Sections below describe details about our lab, including various projects, recent presentations, equipment, and current study sites.Follow our blog posts or send an email to know more!

Current Research Questions

Current projects and questions include:

  • Sundance Snow Monitoring Program: This is a continual monitoring program that compliiments snowpack research at the University of Utah.
  • Dust on Snow: This project features ongoing work and collaboration with the University of Utah to better understand, detect, and integrate the impact of dust on snow in water models.
  • Mapping the Last Ice of Utah: The memory of a past climate survives in hundreds of alpine rock glaciers scattered throughout the state of Utah. With increasing water resource pressure and a changig snowpack, stakeholders in the western US are interested in expanding knowledge of the water budget in the state. One unaccounted for aspect of the water budget includes melt water from rock glaciers. Additionally, updated maps are useful for understanding the distribution of glacier morphology across the state. This project is in collaboration with the University of Utah and the Utah Geological Survey. Two students are involved in a EDMAP mapping project.
  • Fire-snow Interactions: Wildfire is both a natural and human-induced process in the environment. While fire can be important for ecological communities, past management practices and human-induced climate change have caused wildfires to become more intense over the last several decades. This project seeks to understand how wilfire alters the snow-water storage for watersheds in the western US.
  • Glacier Debris Cover Evolution in the Himalaya: Glaciers around the globe are retreating at an accellerated rate, with High Mountain Asia and Himalayan region showing drastic changes in the cryosphere that will impact nearly 2 billion people in the coming century. As glaciers retreat, supraglacial debris can grow over the surface, altering the response to a changing climate. Understanding changes in glacial debris cover and dynamics will provide insight for water resource forecasting in the region.
